EVENT: Boston Marathon 2026 (Apr 20)
HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 56 火山旅 (Lu / The Wanderer) -> CHANGED: 33 天山遯 (Dun / Retreat) (Line 5)
MUTUAL: N/A

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
Fire blazes atop a mountain -- bright, visible, but rootless, passing through rather than settling. The Wanderer depicts a figure in transit, someone far from home navigating unfamiliar terrain with only modest resources. The body trigram is Gen (Mountain/Earth), steady and immovable, while the function trigram is Li (Fire) above it. Fire generates Earth in the five-element cycle, meaning the function produces and supports the body -- a favorable relationship where the visible display actually nourishes the stable foundation below. Yet the overall hexagram theme remains one of transience and impermanence.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body (Gen/Mountain/Earth) is the stable, grounded element -- the enduring structure that outlasts the passing fire. It represents solidity, patience, and the capacity to absorb what the fire offers.
- The function (Li/Fire) generates Earth, actively supporting the body. Fire's brilliance and energy feed into the mountain's substance. This is favorable: the outer display of activity strengthens the inner core.
- The fifth line reads: "射雉一矢亡，終以譽命" -- "Shooting a pheasant, one arrow lost, but in the end gaining honor and reputation." A small sacrifice leads to lasting recognition.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The journey begins with the traveler already on the road. Fire on the mountain is conspicuous but ephemeral -- it illuminates everything around it but cannot stay in one place. The fifth line's imagery of shooting a pheasant and losing an arrow is precise: there is a cost, a sacrifice of something valuable (the arrow), but the prize (the pheasant, and with it reputation) is secured. The transformation into Retreat (Dun, #33) shifts the scene dramatically. Heaven above Mountain -- the vast sky pulling away from the earth, the image of strategic withdrawal. The fifth line of Dun reads: "嘉遯，貞吉" -- "Beautiful retreat, correct and auspicious." This is not flight from defeat but an elegant, well-timed withdrawal that preserves dignity and advantage.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome has the flavor of a pyrrhic-tinged victory -- something is achieved and recognized, but the aftermath involves pulling back rather than pressing forward. The taste is bittersweet: honor earned through sacrifice, followed by a knowing, dignified retreat. There is wisdom in this arc, the quality of someone who understands that winning the moment and withdrawing gracefully is superior to clinging to a position that has already served its purpose.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Fire blazing on a mountaintop -- brilliant, visible from great distances, but unable to remain.
- A pheasant brought down by a single arrow, the arrow itself lost forever -- sacrifice for recognition.
- The vast sky lifting away from the mountain -- a beautiful, deliberate retreat into height and distance.
- A traveler who wins renown at an inn but knows he must move on by morning.
- The mountain standing unchanged after the fire has passed -- endurance beneath spectacle.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The surprise is the movement from visible achievement into retreat. One might expect the traveler who gains honor to press that advantage, but the hexagram counsels the opposite: step back. The Wanderer's general warning -- "旅瑣瑣，斯其所取災" -- is that petty attachments on the road invite disaster. The transformation to Retreat confirms that holding on too tightly would be an error. The favorable element relationship (Fire generating Earth) means the body is well-supported, but the overall narrative arc bends toward withdrawal rather than consolidation. Whatever honor is won must be carried away lightly, not defended at the site of its earning.
