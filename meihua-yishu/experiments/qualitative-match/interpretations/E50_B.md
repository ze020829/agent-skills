HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 25 天雷无妄 (Wu Wang / Innocence) -> CHANGED: 21 火雷噬嗑 (Shi He / Biting Through) (Line 5)
MUTUAL: N/A

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram shows heaven above thunder -- the image of movement in perfect accord with heaven's will, without any falseness or ulterior motive. Wu Wang means "without falseness," the state of pristine innocence and alignment with natural law. The guaci reads "元亨利貞" but adds the crucial caveat: "其匪正有眚，不利有攸往" -- if it is not upright, there will be calamity; it is not favorable to go anywhere. The fifth line, the sovereign position, reads: "无妄之疾，勿藥有喜" -- an illness that comes without wrongdoing on your part; do not medicate, and there will be joy. This is the image of a problem that resolves itself if left alone.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram is Zhen (thunder/wood), representing initiative, ambition, and upward-surging energy. The functioning trigram is Qian (heaven/metal), which in the five-element cycle means metal controls wood -- the active force restrains and cuts the body. This is an unfavorable dynamic for the body, suggesting that external authority or circumstance works against the core ambition.
- The fifth line holds the position of ultimate authority but counsels inaction in the face of apparent trouble. The sovereign who heals by not intervening.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The transformation into hexagram 21, Fire over Thunder (Shi He / Biting Through), introduces the image of a mouth with an obstruction between the teeth that must be bitten through. The guaci says "亨，利用獄" -- success, favorable to use legal proceedings. This is the hexagram of justice, of removing obstacles by decisive force, of chewing through what blocks union. The fifth line of Shi He reads: "噬乾肉，得黃金，貞厲，无咎" -- biting dried meat, obtaining gold; righteous but dangerous, no blame. The arc moves from pristine innocence and trust in natural order to a necessary act of forceful intervention. What begins as a situation requiring patience and non-interference transforms into one demanding that an obstacle be decisively eliminated.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome has the character of unexpected natural justice. Something that initially appears as an undeserved affliction resolves through a decisive, biting action -- not gentle or subtle, but clean and necessary. There is the flavor of dried meat yielding gold when chewed -- hardship that contains hidden value, difficulty that rewards perseverance. The tone is austere, almost judicial, carrying the weight of inevitable reckoning rather than celebration.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Heaven over thunder -- cosmic will expressed through movement, the purity of action aligned with natural law.
- An illness that heals without medicine -- the problem that resolves through patience and trust, not forced intervention.
- A mouth biting through an obstruction -- the decisive moment when the blockage between upper and lower jaws is crushed and union is restored.
- Dried meat yielding gold -- hardship that conceals reward, the value found inside difficulty.
- Lightning and thunder together in the mouth -- the fire and thunder of Shi He, a fierce, illuminating act of consumption.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The metal-controls-wood dynamic warns that the ambitious, upward-surging body will face real restraint from external forces. The fifth line's paradox -- do nothing about the illness and joy will come -- is counterintuitive and easily ignored, but it represents the hexagram's deepest counsel. The surprise is the dramatic shift from passive innocence (Wu Wang) to active, forceful biting (Shi He). The situation demands both patience in the beginning and decisive action in the resolution. Those who medicate the illness prematurely -- who intervene when trust is needed -- will miss the gold hidden inside the dried meat.
