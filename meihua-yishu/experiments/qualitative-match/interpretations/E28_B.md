EVENT: The Masters Golf Tournament
HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 44 天風姤 (Gou / Coming to Meet) -> CHANGED: 28 澤風大過 (Da Guo / Great Excess) (Line 6)
MUTUAL: 1 乾為天 (Qian / The Creative Heaven)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The primary hexagram Gou presents a striking image: a single yin line enters from below, meeting five yang lines above. This is the moment of unexpected encounter -- something feminine, dark, or underestimated pushes upward into a field of overwhelming masculine strength. In the context of a prestigious golf tournament, this suggests a dark horse or unexpected presence entering a field dominated by established powers. The hexagram warns "the woman is bold, do not marry her," indicating that this intruding force is seductive but potentially destabilizing. The atmosphere is charged with the tension of an uninvited guest appearing at a grand gathering.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The single yin line rising from below represents an underdog, a disruptive newcomer, or an overlooked variable that suddenly asserts itself against the established order of five yang lines (the dominant favorites, the tradition-bearers, the expected contenders).
- The mutual hexagram Qian (pure heaven, pure creative force) is extraordinary -- six unbroken yang lines representing maximum creative power, authority, and ambition. This suggests the tournament's inner essence is one of supreme competitive intensity, a field of thoroughbred competitors operating at the highest level of human athletic creativity.
- The changed hexagram Da Guo (Great Excess) indicates a structure under extreme strain -- a ridgepole bending under too much weight. Something has been pushed beyond its natural limits.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The tournament opens with the drama of an unexpected encounter (Gou): something or someone breaks into the established order uninvited. The field is pure creative power (Qian as mutual), every competitor burning with ambition and mastery. But the top line of Gou, the sixth line, delivers the decisive moment: "姤其角，吝，无咎" -- "Meeting at the horn, regret, but no blame." The horn is the uppermost extremity, the very tip -- this is an encounter that happens at the peak, at the final, most exposed point. There is regret or limitation in this meeting (lamentable), but ultimately no fundamental fault. The image suggests that the decisive moment of this tournament occurs at the very apex -- perhaps the final hole, the final putt, the last possible moment -- and it involves a somewhat awkward or regrettable encounter rather than a clean, decisive stroke. The transformation into Da Guo (Great Excess) tells us that this climactic meeting pushes the structure past its breaking point: the ridgepole bends. Something about the outcome is excessive, extraordinary, beyond what the frame was designed to hold.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries an unmistakable flavor of excess and extremity. Da Guo's central image is a beam bowing under impossible weight -- the result of this tournament is something extraordinary, perhaps record-breaking or unprecedented, but also precarious. There is a sense that the final state is not entirely stable, that what was achieved pushed against the boundaries of what seemed possible. The "regret but no blame" of the top line gives the outcome a bittersweet quality -- magnificent yet tinged with something unresolved or lamentable.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- A single dark figure entering a hall of kings -- the uninvited presence that disrupts the expected narrative of the tournament.
- Two rams locking horns at the summit of a cliff -- the decisive encounter happening at the most exposed, elevated, and dangerous point.
- A ridgepole bowing under golden weight -- the tournament structure (records, expectations, tradition) buckling under the magnitude of what occurs.
- Pure heaven (six dragons) -- the competitive field as a sky full of dragons, each one capable of supreme flight.
- A withered poplar sprouting flowers (Da Guo imagery) -- something old and seemingly past its prime producing an unexpected, beautiful, but fragile bloom.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The most striking warning is the transformation into Da Guo (Great Excess): whatever happens at this tournament exceeds normal parameters, and excess always carries structural risk. The encounter "at the horn" (the very tip, the final extremity) suggests the outcome is decided by the narrowest of margins at the very last moment, which may frustrate those hoping for a decisive, comfortable resolution. The presence of Qian as mutual hexagram warns of overwhelming ambition -- the competitive intensity may drive someone past the point of prudence. The surprise is that the single yin line (the underestimated force) is the catalyst for everything: without its intrusion, the story would be unremarkable.
