EVENT: Colombia Parliamentary Election
HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 10 天澤履 (Lu / Treading) -> CHANGED: 1 乾為天 (Qian / The Creative/Heaven) (Line 3)
MUTUAL: 37 風火家人 (Jia Ren / The Family)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram depicts the precarious act of treading on a tiger's tail -- walking alongside overwhelming power with nothing but propriety and careful conduct as protection. Heaven arches above, vast and commanding, while the lake below is joyous but small, pressing upward with cheerful boldness toward something far more powerful than itself. The entire image is one of asymmetric encounter: a lesser force engaging a greater one, where survival depends entirely on comportment, timing, and the quality of one's step. The mood is tense but not hopeless -- the tiger does not bite, provided the treading is done correctly.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The tiger represents an established, overwhelming power structure -- something that could easily destroy the one who approaches but may choose not to, depending on how the approach is conducted. It is authority itself, indifferent but dangerous.
- The one treading is a smaller, perhaps more agile force that relies on joyfulness (the lake's quality), propriety, and correct behavior rather than matching power with power. This actor succeeds by not provoking.
- The mutual hexagram Jia Ren (The Family) introduces domestic order, hierarchical harmony, and the idea that proper roles within a household or institution are the foundation of stability. Each member has a defined place and function.
- The changed hexagram Qian (Pure Heaven/The Creative) represents the ultimate expression of yang force -- pure creative power, undiluted and self-sustaining.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The arc begins with the delicate act of treading. The third line reads: "眇能視，跛能履，履虎尾，咥人，凶。武人為于大君" -- "The one-eyed can see, the lame can walk, but treading on the tiger's tail, one is bitten. Misfortune. A warrior acts as a great lord." This is a severe warning: someone with limited capacity overestimates themselves, attempting what they are not equipped to do. A warrior -- someone defined by force rather than wisdom -- attempts to seize the highest position. The result is being bitten by the tiger. The image is of dangerous overreach, where bravado substitutes for genuine qualification. Yet the transformation into Qian (Pure Heaven) suggests that through this crisis of overreach, the situation resolves into something purely powerful and self-sustaining. The mutual hexagram Jia Ren provides the underlying structural logic: this is ultimately about whether proper order within the household (the institution, the state) is maintained. When roles are respected, the family thrives; when someone steps beyond their station, the tiger bites.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the flavor of a trial by fire that either purifies or destroys. The transformation into pure Qian suggests that what emerges from this treading is something stripped of compromise -- total yang, total creative force, with no yielding yin remaining. This is an outcome of stark clarity: either full empowerment or complete exposure. There is no middle ground in Qian. The resolution eliminates ambiguity and establishes a new hierarchy based on pure strength and creative will, but the path there involves the painful lesson of the third line -- someone gets bitten, someone overreaches, and that failure becomes part of the purification process.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- A one-eyed man claiming he can see perfectly, a lame man insisting he can walk -- the dangerous self-deception of those who overestimate their capabilities in a high-stakes arena.
- Treading on a tiger's tail in a sunlit courtyard -- the danger is visible to everyone except the one who steps forward, buoyed by misplaced confidence.
- A warrior seizing the throne of a great lord -- martial force attempting to occupy a position that requires wisdom and mandate, not merely strength.
- The family household with every member in their proper role -- the kitchen warm, the hearth tended, authority exercised with care rather than violence.
- Six unbroken yang lines (Qian) -- the sky without a single cloud, power absolute and undivided, creative force with nothing to restrain it.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The central warning is unmistakable: the third line describes someone who should not be attempting what they are attempting. "武人為于大君" -- a warrior acting as a great lord -- is the classic image of overreach, of force substituting for legitimacy. The surprise is that this dangerous misstep nevertheless leads to Qian, pure creative heaven. This suggests that the overreach, while immediately punished (the tiger bites), paradoxically clears the way for a purer, more decisive outcome. The bitten warrior becomes either a cautionary tale or, through the ordeal, is transformed into something genuinely sovereign. The mutual hexagram Jia Ren reminds us that the deeper issue is always about proper order within the institution: who belongs where, who speaks for whom, and whether the hierarchy reflects genuine competence or merely inherited position.
