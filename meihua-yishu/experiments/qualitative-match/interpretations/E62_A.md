HEXAGRAM LABEL: A
PRIMARY: 25 天雷无妄 (Wu Wang / Innocence) -> CHANGED: 21 火雷噬嗑 (Shi He / Biting Through) (Line 5)
MUTUAL: (derived from primary)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram presents heaven above thunder -- the natural order in motion, where what moves does so without artifice or calculation. Wu Wang speaks of innocence, sincerity, and the absence of false expectation. Its judgment says "元亨利貞" -- fundamentally prosperous and correct -- but adds a critical qualifier: "其匪正有眚，不利有攸往" -- if not upright there will be calamity, not favorable to go anywhere. The body trigram is Zhen (thunder/wood), representing sudden initiative, movement, and the shock of action. The use trigram is Qian (heaven/metal), representing supreme authority and power. Metal destroys wood in the five-element cycle: the use element actively harms the body. The subject faces an overwhelming, authoritative force that exerts cutting pressure.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Zhen (wood) represents the subject -- energetic, initiating, capable of sudden explosive action, but vulnerable to the cutting force of metal.
- The use trigram Qian (metal) represents the opposing or dominant force -- powerful, authoritative, occupying the position of heaven. This force structurally threatens the body.
- The fifth line occupies the sovereign position. Its yao text reads: "无妄之疾，勿藥有喜" -- "Innocence's illness: do not medicate and there will be joy." This is a paradoxical counsel: the affliction that arises is not to be treated with intervention but allowed to pass on its own. The medicine would be worse than the disease.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The arc begins with Innocence -- a state of natural, unforced action where outcomes flow from sincerity rather than strategy. The fifth line introduces an illness that belongs to this innocent state, a difficulty that arises not from wrongdoing but from the natural order itself. The counsel to not medicate suggests that any attempt to fix or manipulate the situation will only make it worse; the wisest response is patient non-intervention. The transformation into Shi He (Biting Through) dramatically shifts the dynamic: fire above thunder, the image of jaws closing on an obstacle. Shi He is about removing what blocks union -- its judgment says "亨，利用獄" (prosperous, favorable for legal proceedings). The obstacle between the jaws must be bitten through decisively. The movement from non-intervention (do not medicate) to decisive action (bite through) suggests a two-phase process: first endure, then act.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries a compound flavor: initial patience yielding to decisive intervention. The first taste is of restraint in the face of an ailment that seems to demand action -- the discipline of holding back when every instinct says to intervene. The second taste is of sharp, judicial clarity as Shi He's jaws close on the obstacle. The overall sensation is of a problem that resolves itself through a combination of early patience and late decisiveness. There is something clean and final about Shi He -- the obstacle is bitten through and removed, leaving the jaws free to close properly.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- An illness that heals without medicine -- the unexpected affliction that resolves through patience rather than treatment, where intervention would only complicate matters.
- Thunder beneath a clear heaven -- natural force moving without artifice, the honest impulse uncontaminated by calculation.
- Jaws biting through gristle -- the decisive moment when patience gives way to action, the sharp closure that removes the obstacle.
- Lightning illuminating a courtroom -- Shi He's judicial character, where truth is revealed and judgment is rendered in a flash.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The metal-destroys-wood dynamic means the subject faces real pressure from a superior force. The fifth line's "do not medicate" counsel is the key surprise: in a situation of affliction, the instinct to intervene is precisely wrong. The illness passes on its own, and premature treatment creates new problems. The transformation into Biting Through suggests that the real obstacle is not the illness itself but something embedded within the situation that requires decisive removal -- like a bone fragment caught between teeth. The overall warning is against overreaction in the early phase and under-reaction in the later phase. Timing is everything: endure first, then bite decisively when the moment comes.
