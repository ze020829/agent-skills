HEXAGRAM LABEL: A
PRIMARY: 29 坎為水 (Kan / The Abysmal Water) -> CHANGED: 60 水澤節 (Jie / Limitation) (Line 1)
MUTUAL: 27 山雷頤 (Yi / Nourishment)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram presents danger doubled -- water above water, abyss upon abyss, the image of falling into a pit only to find another pit beneath it. Kan is the hexagram of repeated peril, where each challenge leads not to solid ground but to another challenge. Yet the hexagram text affirms: "有孚，維心亨，行有尚" -- there is sincerity within, the heart finds its way through, and action is honored. This is not despair but the recognition that some endeavors must pass through genuine danger, and that maintaining inner truth is the only compass when the terrain offers no footholds. The water flows ever downward, filling every hollow it encounters, never stopping until it finds its level.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The double abyss represents a situation where risk is layered -- the venture faces not one obstacle but a sequence of them, and the temptation is to believe the first difficulty is the last, when in reality the path descends further.
- Water's nature is to flow sincerely, filling every space without pretense. This quality of authentic, unhesitating commitment is the key resource available.
- The mutual hexagram Yi (Nourishment) introduces the image of the mouth -- what goes in, what comes out, what feeds and what is consumed. The jaws of the mountain (above) and thunder (below) suggest both appetite and the selective act of choosing what to take in.
- The changed hexagram Jie (Limitation) brings the discipline of measured constraint -- the lake held within bounds by the water above it. Not everything should flow without limit; boundaries create value.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The situation begins in doubled peril -- the venture is launched into genuinely uncertain, dangerous terrain. The first line reads: "習坎，入于坎窞，凶" -- "Repeated abysses, falling into the pit within the pit, misfortune." This is the most ominous position: the very first step plunges into the deepest part of the danger. There is no gentle entry here -- the initial experience is one of falling, of discovering that the bottom is deeper than expected. The word "凶" (misfortune) is unambiguous. Yet the transformation into Jie (Limitation) reveals that this initial plunge leads to the recognition that boundaries are necessary. The abyss cannot be crossed by pouring oneself out without limit; instead, one must learn where to stop, where to conserve, where to establish banks and dams. The mutual hexagram Yi (Nourishment) anchors the deeper question: what does this venture actually feed? What nourishment does it provide, and to whom? The jaws must be selective -- not everything that enters the mouth sustains, and not everything produced satisfies genuine hunger.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the flavor of a sobering initial encounter with difficulty that teaches the value of limitation. What begins as a plunge into the abyss resolves into the discipline of measured constraint -- the recognition that sustainable success requires boundaries rather than unlimited flow. The taste is of cold water: bracing, clarifying, and ultimately necessary, but not immediately pleasant. There is wisdom gained through the experience of falling, and the limitation that follows is not defeat but the beginning of a more sustainable relationship with the terrain.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Falling into a pit within a pit -- the dizzying discovery that the ground beneath the danger is itself dangerous, that the challenge has layers not visible from above.
- Water flowing into every crevice, sincere and unstoppable but also cold and indiscriminate -- the venture's authentic quality seeking its natural level regardless of what that level turns out to be.
- Jaws opening and closing (Yi) -- the act of consumption, of appetite and selectivity, the question of what this creation feeds and whether the audience is truly hungry for it.
- A lake bounded by its banks (Jie) -- the beauty and utility that emerge when limitless flow is given structure, when the water is contained rather than spilled.
- A traveler who steps confidently into what appears to be a shallow stream only to find it chest-deep -- the shock of underestimated depth.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The first line's warning is severe and direct: "入于坎窞，凶" -- entering the pit within the pit is misfortune. This suggests that the initial reception or opening experience will be more difficult than anticipated, a deeper plunge than anyone prepared for. The surprise is that this plunge leads not to drowning but to Limitation (Jie), whose hexagram text says "亨" (success) -- limitation itself is the path to viability. The counterintuitive lesson is that the venture's long-term health depends on accepting constraints rather than trying to fill every available space. The mutual hexagram Yi adds an unexpected dimension: the question is ultimately about nourishment -- whether this offering genuinely feeds something in its audience or merely enters the mouth without sustaining. "舍爾靈龜，觀我朵頤，凶" -- Yi's first line warns against abandoning one's own spiritual resources to gawk at someone else's feast, a caution about superficial consumption over genuine sustenance.
