EVENT: NASA Artemis II Crewed Lunar Flyby Mission
HEXAGRAM LABEL: A
PRIMARY: 48 水風井 (Jing / The Well) -> CHANGED: 46 地風升 (Sheng / Pushing Upward) (Line 5)
MUTUAL: 38 火澤睽 (Kui / Opposition)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The primary hexagram Jing (The Well) presents one of the most enduring images in the Yijing: a well that nourishes a community, unchanging even as towns and peoples around it shift. "You may change the town, but you cannot change the well." In the context of a crewed lunar flyby mission, this depicts the deep, inexhaustible source of human aspiration and capability that space programs draw upon -- a well of knowledge, engineering tradition, and collective ambition that has been accumulating since the earliest days of spaceflight. The well does not move; the mission draws from it. The hexagram's warning is about the vessel: if the bucket breaks before reaching the surface, the water is lost.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- Water over wind (Kan over Xun): water drawn upward by the penetrating action of wind beneath. The engineering effort of a lunar mission is precisely this -- drawing precious capability upward from deep reserves through persistent, penetrating effort. The wind below is the patient, systematic work of engineers; the water above is the mission capability that this effort brings to the surface.
- The mutual hexagram Kui (Opposition) introduces internal tension: fire and lake moving in opposite directions, estrangement and divergence within the project. Different teams, different priorities, different visions pulling against each other even as the common goal unites them.
- The changed hexagram Sheng (Pushing Upward) is earth over wind -- gentle, persistent ascent. The wood grows upward through the earth, rising without resistance. This is the image of successful, organic elevation.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The mission draws from the deep well of accumulated spaceflight knowledge and capability. The infrastructure is ancient in institutional terms -- decades of experience, proven systems, established protocols. The fifth line of Jing delivers a luminous message: "井冽，寒泉食" -- "The well is clear and cold, its spring water fit to drink." This is the sovereign line of the Well hexagram, and its meaning is pure: the source is clean, the water is good, and it can nourish those who draw from it. In the context of a lunar mission, this says that the core capability -- the engineering, the hardware, the human readiness -- is sound. The well has been cleared, the water runs clean and cold, and it is ready to sustain the mission. Through the mutual hexagram Kui (Opposition), we see internal tensions: fire and water pulling apart, different elements within the program that do not naturally agree. Yet Kui is also about finding unity through acknowledged difference -- the hexagram says "small matters, auspicious." The divergent forces can be reconciled at a practical level even if they cannot fully harmonize. The transformation into Sheng (Pushing Upward) is deeply apt for a space mission: gentle, persistent ascent from earth into sky. The wood pushes through soil toward light -- the spacecraft pushes through atmosphere toward the moon. Sheng promises that this upward movement meets no fundamental obstruction.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the flavor of clear, nourishing success achieved through patient upward movement. It is not dramatic or explosive but organic and steady, like a plant growing toward light or clean water rising from a deep source. The "cold spring fit to drink" gives the outcome a quality of purity and fundamental soundness -- whatever this mission achieves, it will be genuine and sustaining rather than superficial. Sheng's gentle persistence suggests the mission progresses through careful, incremental steps rather than daring leaps.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- A deep stone well with clear, ice-cold water visible at the bottom -- the accumulated capability of decades of spaceflight, pure and ready to be drawn upon.
- A rope and bucket descending into darkness, then rising with precious water -- the spacecraft descending through Earth's gravity well and rising toward lunar orbit, carrying its precious human cargo.
- A tree pushing through dark soil toward sunlight (Sheng) -- the persistent, organic upward thrust of the mission through layers of difficulty toward its luminous destination.
- Fire and lake pulling in opposite directions (Kui) -- the internal tensions of a massive engineering project where different systems and teams must somehow converge.
- A town rebuilt many times around the same unchanging well -- the space program itself, restructured and renamed across decades, but always drawing from the same deep source of human aspiration.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The Well hexagram carries a specific, haunting warning in its judgment: "汔至，亦未繘井，羸其瓶，凶" -- "Almost there, but the rope does not quite reach the water; the pitcher breaks -- misfortune." This warns that the greatest danger is not at the beginning or middle but at the very end, when success seems within reach. A broken vessel at the last moment could waste all the effort of drawing from the well. For a lunar flyby mission, this is a pointed caution about the final phases -- reentry, splashdown, the last critical maneuvers. The surprise is the gentle optimism of the fifth line's clear spring: the source itself is sound, the fundamental capability is there. The risk lies not in the water but in the vessel that carries it.
