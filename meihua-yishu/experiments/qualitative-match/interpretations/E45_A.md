1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The image is of heaven contained within the mountain -- enormous creative power held in check by the stillness of stone. The body trigram is Qian (Heaven/Metal), representing pure creative force, ambition, and strength. The function trigram is Gen (Mountain/Earth), representing restraint and accumulation. In five-element terms, Earth generates Metal -- the function actively nourishes the body, feeding the creative power with ever-greater reserves. This is the hexagram of vast stored energy, the great reservoir before the dam opens, the drawn bow before release. The dictum "不家食吉" -- "not eating at home is auspicious" -- suggests the accumulated power is meant to serve beyond the household.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body (Qian/Heaven/Metal) is sheer creative potency -- the strongest, most yang force in the system, brimming with initiative and authority.
- The function (Gen/Mountain/Earth) generates Metal, meaning the restraining force is simultaneously building up the very power it contains. This is accumulation at its most productive: the more you hold back, the more you gain.
- The top line reads: "何天之衢，亨" -- "What a highway of heaven, success!" This is the moment the accumulated power finds its thoroughfare, the bottleneck opens, and everything that was stored pours forth into the open road.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The arc is classical: great accumulation reaching its apex and then releasing into peace. The top line is the culmination -- the sixth position, where things reach their extreme and transform. "何天之衢" is an exclamation of amazement at the vast road that suddenly appears. All the pent-up energy of Great Accumulation finds an unobstructed path. The transformation into Tai (Peace, #11) is one of the most auspicious possible outcomes in the Yijing. Tai represents heaven and earth in perfect exchange, "小往大來" -- the small goes out and the great comes in. It is the hexagram of spring, of opening, of barriers dissolved and communication restored. From hoarding to flowing, from containment to exchange -- the transition could not be more dramatic or more welcome.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome is expansive and deeply positive. It carries the feeling of floodgates opening into fertile plains, of a long winter giving way to the first warm day of spring. There is exhilaration in the release and a sense of cosmic rightness -- heaven and earth finally aligned, the stored wealth finally distributed, the creative power finally deployed where it can do the most good. The flavor is generous, abundant, and publicly celebratory.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Heaven contained inside a mountain, vast creative power held in geological patience, until the road to heaven suddenly opens.
- A dam bursting not destructively but productively, stored waters flowing into dry fields.
- The highway of heaven -- "何天之衢" -- an expression of wonder at the scope of the path now available.
- Spring arriving after long winter -- "小往大來," the small departs and the great arrives.
- A storehouse flung open, its accumulated wealth now flowing outward to nourish the world.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The primary surprise is at the top line: the accumulation does not merely trickle out but explodes into "the highway of heaven." This is a maximal release. The warning embedded in Tai is its own shadow: "无平不陂，无往不復" -- no flat ground without a slope, nothing that goes out without returning. Peace, once achieved, must be vigilantly maintained, because the cycle will eventually turn toward its opposite (Pi/Stagnation, #12). But in the moment of this reading, the trajectory is overwhelmingly favorable: stored power finds its road, restraint transforms into generosity, and the resulting peace is deep and genuine. The only caution is against assuming this abundance is permanent.
