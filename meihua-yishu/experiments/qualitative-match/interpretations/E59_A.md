HEXAGRAM LABEL: A
PRIMARY: 44 天風姤 (Gou / Encounter) -> CHANGED: 28 澤風大過 (Da Guo / Great Exceeding) (Line 6)
MUTUAL: (derived from primary)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram depicts an unexpected encounter -- heaven above, wind below, a single yin line entering from beneath five yang lines. The image is of something uninvited slipping in at the foundation, a powerful feminine force meeting an overwhelmingly masculine structure. The hexagram's judgment warns "女壯，勿用取女" -- the woman is strong, do not take this woman -- suggesting that what arrives unexpectedly carries a disruptive potency that should not be underestimated. The body trigram is Xun (wind/wood), representing penetration and flexibility, while the use trigram is Qian (heaven/metal). Metal cuts wood in the five-element cycle: the use element actively harms the body. This is a fundamentally adversarial configuration.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Xun (wood) represents the entity whose fate we track -- adaptable, communicative, penetrating, but ultimately vulnerable to being cut.
- The use trigram Qian (heaven/metal) represents an overwhelming, powerful force -- authoritative, unyielding, and in a controlling position. Metal destroying wood suggests that the dominant external force exerts cutting pressure on the subject.
- The top line (line 6) is the moving line, occupying the ultimate position. Its yao text reads: "姤其角，吝，无咎" -- "Encountering at the horns, regret, but no disaster." This is the image of meeting at the highest, most rigid point -- the tips of the horns -- a collision that is embarrassing or limiting but not catastrophic.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The arc begins with Encounter -- something unexpected arrives and must be reckoned with. The top line indicates this encounter occurs at the very extreme, at the apex where things are most rigid and least flexible. "Encountering at the horns" suggests a head-to-head meeting that is awkward and constrained, like two rams locking horns at the tips with no room to maneuver. The transformation into Da Guo (Great Exceeding) is dramatic: the ridgepole bends under excessive weight ("棟橈"). Four yang lines are compressed between two yin lines at top and bottom, creating a structure that is overburdened at its center. The image is of a beam sagging dangerously, a structure pushed beyond its normal capacity. Yet Da Guo also says "利有攸往，亨" -- it is favorable to have somewhere to go, suggesting that bold, extraordinary action can turn crisis into passage.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome has the flavor of something pushed to its structural limit -- a bridge groaning under too much weight, a framework bending but not yet breaking. There is regret and awkwardness in the encounter (吝), but not ruin (无咎). The sensation is of overextension, of an entity asked to bear more than its normal design allows. There is a paradoxical opportunity within the excess: extraordinary circumstances demand extraordinary measures, and those who act decisively may find passage through the crisis.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Two rams meeting at the horn tips -- a rigid, awkward collision at the highest point, with no room to yield or retreat.
- A ridgepole bending under excessive load -- the central structure sagging visibly, stressed beyond its intended capacity.
- Wind meeting an iron sky -- the flexible, penetrating force of Xun encountering the unyielding ceiling of Qian, unable to pass through.
- An uninvited guest at the gate -- the yin line slipping in at the base of Gou, something arriving that was not planned for and cannot easily be expelled.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The metal-destroys-wood dynamic is the central warning: the body is under active pressure from the use element, meaning the subject faces genuine structural harm from the forces it encounters. The top line's "regret but no disaster" offers some consolation -- the damage is to pride and flexibility rather than to survival. The surprise is the transformation into Great Exceeding, which suggests the aftermath involves bearing loads far beyond normal expectations. Da Guo's counsel is to act boldly ("利有攸往") rather than trying to shore up the sagging structure with timid measures. The overall warning is against rigidity: meeting at the horns produces only friction, while the bending ridgepole demands creative, unconventional responses.
