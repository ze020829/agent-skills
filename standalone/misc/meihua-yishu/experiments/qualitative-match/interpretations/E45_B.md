HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 6 天水訟 (Song / Conflict) -> CHANGED: 10 天澤履 (Lu / Treading) (Line 1)
MUTUAL: N/A

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
Heaven above, Water below -- two forces moving in opposite directions. Heaven ascends, Water descends, and between them a fundamental disagreement about direction and purpose. This is the hexagram of litigation, of dispute, of two parties who cannot reconcile their movements. The body trigram is Qian (Heaven/Metal), representing authority, strength, and creative drive. The function trigram is Kan (Water), which drains Metal in the five-element cycle -- Water exhausts the body's reserves, sapping its strength through persistent, flowing pressure. The overall dictum is stark: "有孚窒惕，中吉，終凶" -- sincerity is blocked, be alert; middle fortune, but the end is ominous.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body (Qian/Heaven/Metal) is the authoritative force, seeking to assert itself and lead. It is powerful but being drained by Water's relentless pull.
- The function (Kan/Water) exhausts Metal, meaning the opposing force or environmental conditions are steadily depleting the body's strength. This is not a dramatic attack but a slow erosion -- water wearing down metal.
- The first line reads: "不永所事，小有言，終吉" -- "Do not prolong the affair, there will be small criticism, but in the end auspicious." The counsel at the very beginning is to avoid drawn-out engagement, to cut the dispute short.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The situation opens with inherent tension: two forces pulling apart, sincerity unable to reach its target, the need for constant vigilance. The first line's advice is to abandon the dispute early -- accept minor criticism rather than persist in a confrontation that will only worsen over time. The transformation into Treading (Lu, #10) reframes the scene entirely. From open conflict, the hexagram shifts to the image of "履虎尾，不咥人" -- treading on a tiger's tail without being bitten. This is the art of navigating danger through propriety and careful comportment. The lake (Dui/Metal) now sits beneath Heaven (Qian/Metal), and the body element remains strong. The transition from Conflict to Treading is a movement from confrontation to careful navigation -- from fighting the tiger to walking beside it.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome has the quality of tension managed rather than tension resolved. There is no triumphant victory over the dispute, no crushing of the opponent -- instead, there is the taut, careful satisfaction of having walked through danger without being harmed. The flavor is one of alert caution rewarded, of skill in reading the terrain and adjusting one's step. The tiger does not bite, but it is still a tiger, and the awareness of its presence never fully dissipates.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Heaven and Water pulling in opposite directions -- an irreconcilable argument of direction embedded in nature itself.
- Water slowly eroding metal -- persistent depletion rather than sudden destruction.
- A tiger's tail underfoot -- the moment of maximum danger navigated through propriety rather than force.
- A lawsuit abandoned early -- "不永所事" -- the wisdom of knowing when to stop arguing.
- Walking on thin ice beside a predator, each step deliberate and measured.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The most pointed warning is the hexagram dictum itself: "中吉，終凶" -- the middle is auspicious but the end is ominous. This means that early and moderate engagement may yield decent results, but prolonged pursuit will end badly. The transformation into Treading offers a way out -- by shifting from confrontation to careful navigation, the worst outcome can be avoided. The surprise is that the first line is actually the most favorable position in the entire hexagram of Conflict, precisely because it counsels immediate withdrawal. The element dynamic of Water draining Metal means the body cannot sustain a prolonged contest; its reserves are being steadily consumed. Speed and brevity are essential.
