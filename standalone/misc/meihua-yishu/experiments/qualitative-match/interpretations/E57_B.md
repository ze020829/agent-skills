HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 24 地雷復 (Fu / Return) -> CHANGED: 27 山雷頤 (Yi / Nourishment) (Line 6)
BODY=震(木), FUNCTION=坤(土)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The image is of a single yang line stirring at the very bottom of five yin lines -- thunder returning beneath the earth, the first pulse of new life after a long dormancy. Fu (Return) is the hexagram of renewal, of the winter solstice when the days begin to lengthen again, of the first green shoot breaking through frozen soil. The structure is overwhelmingly yin with one decisive yang presence emerging from below. The sixth line -- the very top, the final position of the yin sequence -- transforms the upper trigram from earth (Kun) into mountain (Gen), yielding Yi (Nourishment), the hexagram shaped like an open mouth, concerned with what one takes in and what one gives out.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Zhen (Wood) is nourished by the function trigram Kun (Earth). Earth supports wood -- trees root in soil and draw sustenance from it. This is a genuinely supportive relationship; the passive, receptive ground feeds the vital, growing force beneath it. The body is well-nourished by the function.
- The sixth line of Fu is the most extreme yin position in a hexagram about yang's return -- it represents the point most distant from the renewal happening below, the corner where darkness still holds sway.
- Yi (Nourishment) as the changed hexagram introduces the image of the jaw and the question of proper nourishment: what do you feed on, and what feeds on you?

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The sixth line reads: "迷復，凶，有災眚。用行師，終有大敗，以其國君凶，至于十年不克征" -- "Lost in return, misfortune, there are disasters and errors. Using the army results in final great defeat; for the ruler of the nation, misfortune; for ten years unable to mount campaigns." This is among the harshest yao texts in the entire Yi Jing. The figure at the top has missed the moment of return entirely -- while the yang seed stirs far below, this position remains lost in accumulated darkness. The transformation into Yi (Nourishment) then reframes this catastrophic failure as a question of sustenance: the great defeat arises from feeding on the wrong things, from taking in poison rather than nourishment. Yi's structure -- a mouth with an open center -- asks whether the jaw has been biting on what truly sustains or on what destroys.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the heavy, sobering taste of a missed opportunity and its long consequences. It is the feeling of winter lingering in one place while spring has already begun elsewhere -- of being the last to turn, the last to adapt, and paying dearly for that delay. The ten-year prohibition on campaigns suggests not a brief setback but a structural limitation, a wound that takes a generation to heal. Yi's presence as the changed hexagram offers a path forward through the most basic question: what are you nourishing yourself with? The recovery, when it comes, begins not with action but with correcting what one consumes.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- A single green shoot breaking through frozen earth -- the yang returning, but far from the frozen top where the sixth line dwells.
- A general returning from a devastating campaign, ten years before another march is possible -- the scope and duration of the loss.
- An open mouth, the jaw of Yi -- the fundamental question of what sustains and what poisons.
- Thunder buried under earth and mountain -- the vital force present but deeply covered, requiring patience to emerge.
- The winter solstice missed -- the moment of turning passed unnoticed by one who was looking the wrong way.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The warning is stark and direct: this is one of the most explicitly unfavorable yao texts in the Yi Jing. The "ten years unable to campaign" speaks of consequences that extend far beyond the immediate moment. The surprise is that earth genuinely supports wood -- the five-element relationship is favorable, meaning the environment itself is nourishing. The failure comes not from hostile circumstances but from the figure's own inability to read the moment of return. Yi as the changed hexagram offers the only consolation: even after devastating loss, the path back begins with the simple, humble act of nourishing oneself correctly -- choosing what to consume, what to absorb, what to reject.
