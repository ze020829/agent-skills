HEXAGRAM LABEL: A
PRIMARY: 34 雷天大壯 (Da Zhuang / Great Strength) -> CHANGED: 54 雷澤歸妹 (Gui Mei / The Marrying Maiden) (Line 3)
BODY=震(木), FUNCTION=乾(金)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The image is of thunder roaring above heaven -- raw power shaking the highest reaches of the sky. Da Zhuang (Great Strength) depicts a moment of maximum yang energy, four solid lines pressing upward with irresistible force. The structure trembles with potency; the ram lowers its horns and charges. The third line sits at the threshold between the lower trigram (Qian/Heaven) and the upper (Zhen/Thunder) -- the most dangerous position, where inner force meets outer expression. Its movement transforms the lower trigram from heaven into lake, yielding Gui Mei (The Marrying Maiden), a hexagram of subordinate union, of the younger sister given in marriage -- an arrangement tinged with inevitability and lack of choice.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Zhen (Wood) is acted upon by the function trigram Qian (Metal/Gold). Metal chops wood: the function directly harms the body. The powerful, creative force of heaven attacks the vital, growing force of thunder. This is a relationship of genuine threat -- the body's vitality is under siege from the very strength it relies on.
- The third line is classically the most perilous position -- "多凶多懼" (much misfortune, much fear). It represents the moment of transition where internal pressure becomes external action, often with reckless consequences.
- Gui Mei (The Marrying Maiden) as the changed hexagram carries an inherently unfavorable judgment: "征凶，无攸利" -- "undertakings bring misfortune, nothing is favorable."

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The third line reads: "小人用壯，君子用罔，貞厲。羝羊觸藩，羸其角" -- "The small person uses strength; the superior person does not. Perseverance is dangerous. The ram butts against a hedge and gets its horns entangled." This is a vivid image of power misapplied -- the ram charges with all its might, only to become trapped in the very barrier it tried to break through. Strength defeats itself. The transformation into Gui Mei then recasts this failed assault as an arranged, possibly unwanted union. The maiden is given in marriage not by her own choice but by circumstance and obligation. The narrative arc moves from explosive, overconfident assertion to a humbling accommodation -- the ram that charged the fence must now accept the terms of its entanglement.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the taste of deflation after a spectacular charge -- the moment when raw power discovers it cannot simply batter through every obstacle. There is something rueful and even humiliating in the transformation: from the roaring thunder of Da Zhuang to the subordinate position of the marrying maiden. Gui Mei's character is one of making the best of a situation one did not choose, of finding dignity within constraint. The flavor is of a powerful force brought to a negotiated settlement, accepting less than it demanded.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- A great ram charging a hedge and catching its horns -- power that traps itself through its own aggression.
- Thunder above heaven -- an overwhelming display of force that shakes everything but ultimately spends itself.
- The younger sister given in marriage -- an arrangement driven by obligation rather than desire, a union of unequal parties.
- Horns tangled in branches -- the specific, painful image of strength that cannot advance or retreat.
- Metal chopping wood -- the body's vitality cut down by the very force it engaged.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The central warning is against the misuse of strength. The third line explicitly distinguishes between the small person's approach (using brute force) and the superior person's approach (restraint through wisdom). The ram's entangled horns are a warning that charging headlong leads to paralysis, not breakthrough. Gui Mei's unfavorable judgment ("nothing is favorable") suggests that the resulting arrangement satisfies no one fully. The surprise may be how quickly overwhelming strength transforms into subordinate accommodation -- the distance from Da Zhuang to Gui Mei is the distance from the battlefield to the bridal chamber, and the journey is neither voluntary nor comfortable.
