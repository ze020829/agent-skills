EVENT: Cape Verde Parliamentary Election (May 17)
HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 36 地火明夷 (Ming Yi / Darkening of the Light) -> CHANGED: 63 水火既濟 (Ji Ji / After Completion) (Line 5)
BODY=離(火), FUNCTION=坤(土)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
The image is of fire buried beneath the earth -- brightness driven underground, the sun sinking below the horizon. Ming Yi (Darkening of the Light) speaks of a time when brilliance must be concealed, when the luminous must operate in obscurity, when those with vision are forced to dim their own light to survive. The earth presses down upon the flame, smothering it without quite extinguishing it. The fifth line -- the sovereign position -- holds a special resonance in this hexagram, invoking the legendary Jizi (箕子), the wise prince who feigned madness to survive under a tyrant. Its movement transforms the upper trigram from earth (Kun) into water (Kan), yielding Ji Ji (After Completion) -- the perfectly balanced hexagram where every line sits in its correct position.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Li (Fire) generates the function trigram Kun (Earth). Fire produces earth (ash) in the five-element cycle, meaning the body exhausts itself to feed the function. The bright, illuminating force gives away its energy to the heavy, absorbing ground above it -- a draining, self-sacrificing dynamic.
- The fifth line is the sovereign's seat, but in Ming Yi it is occupied by Jizi -- the wise advisor who cannot act openly and must protect his inner light through concealment.
- Ji Ji (After Completion) as the changed hexagram presents the image of perfect order: water above fire, the pot on the stove, everything in its right place. But Ji Ji's own warning is "初吉終亂" -- initially auspicious, eventually chaotic.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The fifth line reads: "箕子之明夷，利貞" -- "The darkening of the light as experienced by Jizi; perseverance is favorable." Jizi was the uncle of the tyrant Zhou of Shang, who saw the kingdom's corruption clearly but could not stop it. He chose to conceal his brilliance and endure. The counsel is to maintain inner integrity while outwardly dimming one's presence. The transformation into Ji Ji then presents a startling contrast: from concealment and suffering to the appearance of perfect completion. Everything seems to arrive at its proper place -- water sits above fire, yin and yang alternate perfectly. But Ji Ji's foundational warning haunts this resolution: what looks complete is already beginning to unravel.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome carries the bittersweet flavor of apparent completion masking deeper instability. It is the feeling of a beautiful sunset -- gorgeous in its arrangement, but signaling the approach of night. Ji Ji's perfect order is inherently fragile; the very completeness of the arrangement means any movement from this point is a movement toward disorder. The taste is of a meal perfectly cooked that begins cooling the moment it is served. There is real achievement here, real order, but it exists at a peak from which descent is inevitable.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Fire buried beneath earth -- brilliance forced underground, the wise advisor hiding in plain sight.
- Jizi feigning madness -- the most extreme form of strategic concealment, protecting inner truth through outward degradation.
- A perfectly balanced pot on a stove -- water above fire, everything in its correct position, the ideal moment of completion.
- The eastern neighbor's lavish ox-sacrifice compared to the western neighbor's simple offering -- Ji Ji's fifth line teaches that sincerity matters more than spectacle.
- A sunset in perfect colors -- the beauty of completion that contains within it the certainty of darkness.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The primary warning is Ji Ji's "初吉終亂" -- initial good fortune, eventual disorder. Whatever completion is achieved is inherently temporary and will begin to degrade. The surprise is the transformation from darkness (Ming Yi) to completion (Ji Ji) -- one expects concealment to lead to further obscurity, but instead it produces perfect order. The deeper warning is that fire exhausting itself to feed earth is not sustainable; the body's brightness is being consumed. The fifth line's counsel of perseverance (利貞) suggests that the only viable strategy is endurance and inner constancy, not dramatic action.
