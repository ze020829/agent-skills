EVENT: Colombia Presidential Election First Round (May 31)
HEXAGRAM LABEL: B
PRIMARY: 10 天澤履 (Lu / Treading) -> CHANGED: 1 乾為天 (Qian / The Creative) (Line 3)
MUTUAL: (derived from primary)

1. SITUATION DESCRIPTION
This hexagram presents heaven above lake -- the image of treading on a tiger's tail. Lu is fundamentally about navigating danger through proper conduct, walking a perilous path with such correctness of behavior that the tiger does not bite. The judgment says "履虎尾，不咥人，亨" -- treading on the tiger's tail, it does not bite, success. The body trigram is Qian (heaven/metal), representing supreme creative power, authority, and strength. The use trigram is Dui (lake/metal), also representing metal -- joy, openness, and exchange. Both trigrams are metal: body and use share the same element, creating a situation of fundamental kinship between the forces involved, with neither generating nor destroying the other.

2. KEY ACTORS / FORCES
- The body trigram Qian (metal) represents the sovereign, the ultimate authority -- powerful, creative, and occupying the position of heaven.
- The use trigram Dui (metal) represents a joyful, open force that approaches the sovereign from below. Same element, different expression: one is unyielding heaven, the other is a smiling lake.
- The third line is the moving line, sitting at the top of the lower trigram, the most precarious transitional position. Its yao text reads: "眇能視，跛能履，履虎尾，咥人，凶。武人為于大君" -- "The one-eyed can see, the lame can walk, but treading on the tiger's tail, bitten, misfortune. A warrior acts as a great ruler." This is the most dangerous line in Lu: someone with limited capacity overestimates their ability, a military figure usurps sovereign authority.

3. PROCESS NARRATIVE
The arc begins with Treading -- careful navigation of dangerous terrain. But the third line shatters the hexagram's reassurance: here the tiger does bite. The one-eyed person who insists they can see, the lame person who insists they can walk -- these are images of self-deception, of capabilities overstated. The "武人為于大君" image is particularly pointed: a warrior seizing the role of great ruler, military force assuming political authority it was not designed to hold. The transformation into Pure Qian (The Creative) means all six lines become yang -- absolute, total creative power without any yielding element. This is the dragon in all its phases: submerged, emerging, vigilant, leaping, flying, and ultimately overreaching ("亢龍有悔"). The movement from Lu to Qian suggests the treading figure abandons caution entirely and claims total authority.

4. QUALITATIVE OUTCOME CHARACTER
The outcome has the flavor of a dangerous overreach dressed in the garments of supreme authority. What begins as careful navigation of peril transforms into a claim of absolute power. The taste is of ambition unchecked -- the lame walker who insists on running, the warrior who seats himself on the throne. Pure Qian is magnificent in its creative power but contains within it the inevitable "亢龍有悔" (the arrogant dragon has regret) at its zenith. The overall character is one of a bold gambit that may achieve momentary dominance but carries the seeds of overextension.

5. SPECIFIC IMAGERY / METAPHORS
- Treading on a tiger's tail and being bitten -- the one moment where proper conduct fails and the tiger turns, the breach of protocol that invites retaliation.
- A one-eyed man claiming perfect sight -- self-deception about capabilities, the insistence on acting beyond one's actual capacity.
- A warrior seating himself on the throne -- military force assuming political authority, the dangerous substitution of strength for legitimacy.
- Six dragons ascending in sequence -- Pure Qian's grand arc from hidden potential to soaring flight to inevitable regret, the full cycle of unchecked ambition.

6. WARNINGS / SURPRISES
The third line's warning is unambiguous: "凶" -- misfortune. This is the only line in Lu where the tiger bites, and it bites precisely because the person treading lacks the capacity they claim to possess. The "武人為于大君" warning about a warrior usurping sovereign authority is the hexagram's most pointed political image. The transformation into Pure Qian is simultaneously magnificent and dangerous: total yang contains no yin check, no yielding element, no self-correction mechanism. The metal-metal dynamic between body and use means the forces are fundamentally alike -- there is kinship but also rivalry between identical natures, each claiming the same ground. The overall warning is against mistaking force for fitness and boldness for legitimacy.
